Predicting Championship Title Wins in League of Legends

Introduction¶

League of Legends (often referred to as League), in its most played gamemode is an online 5 versus 5 Multiplayer Online Battle Arena (MOBA) game that has attracted millions of fans all of the world since its realease in 2009. The game hosts a roster of over 150 unique playable characters, or champions, each having their own unique skills and abilities for fans to pilot. Entering the game, each team of five is split into different roles across a map consisting of three "lanes" that players can march down to do battle, with large dark "jungle" separating each lane. The overall goal of each match of League of Legends is to march down a lane, defeat your opponents in battle, and destroy the enemy structures blocking each lane. A team wins once they are the first to destroy all the enemy structures in at least one lane, reaching the opponent's base, reffered to as the nexus, and destroy it to end the game.

The MOBA genre began as a user created gamemode for the popular Real Time Strategy (RTS) game StarCraft. In this mode, players controlled a strong character that would march alongside weaker computer controlled units to do battle against a powerful computer controlled enemy. This idea inspired a similar fanmade gamemode for another RTS called Warcraft III. This second fanmade gamemode was called Defense of the Ancients (DotA), and it was this game that lead to the massively popular sequel DotA II along with DotA's spiritual sequel: League of Legends.

League of Legends and DotA II have developed into serious competitors in the online gaming market, and have risen to become some of the most played games year after year for roughly the past decade. They have grown to the point where large professional scenes have risen for each game, with League of Legends being one of the most popular eSports ever. The League professional scene consists of 12 different regions across the world that host a multitude of teams playing regular matches on a weekly basis. The year is split into two seasons called "splits": a Spring split and a Summer split. Each region ends their splits deciding one team to be the champion for that split, and then twice a year the top teams from each region come together to compete in massive global tournaments. These tournaments include the Mid Season Invitational ("MSI", occuring after Spring) and the much larger World Championship ("Worlds", hosted after Summer). The two global tournaments garner millions of viewers and offer millions of dollars in prize money to teams that succeed. In 2019 and 2020, the World Championship events peaked at 44 and 45 million concurrent viewers respectively. League of Legends eSports events have given over $60 million in prize money to date (Gough) and in some regions teams have been franchised for in the millions of dollars.

In this overall analysis, we wish to garner insights through analyzing data collected in a large dataset of almost every professional League of Legends match played in the years 2015 to 2018. With this data, we can potentially notice trends in which specific teams or regions win the most. We may also be able to use match data to predict which team would win each of the major regional or global tournament events.

Insights Gained¶

Analyzing the methods and results of our created model, I would argue that the qualification used for the "top team" would definitely have to change. The goal of the analysis was to predict how teams would do in the next major tournament, so it seemed to make sense to pick whoever wins the most as the best team. However, we see from our data that different leagues play different number of games so this might not be an accurate way to judge who is the best. We also might want to find ways to include many more attribute that were listed above. For example, Gold is a very important part of the game, and Gold Differences are often how casters and fans judge who is winning a game while it is live. While the dataset's format might have made averaging gold differences somewhat difficult, either retrieving this information from another source or wrangling from the dataset would likely have proved very useful. Overall, there was some success in predicting the tournament success of teams from the dataset, however it is clear that you can never truly tell how all the teams will do. This makes sense, however, since there would be no need to play the games out if we could already tell who was going to win.
